When fitting any model more complicated than a straight line, it is wise to specify an initial set of parameters to the fitting routine which are in the right range to describe the data. So, let's estimate the parameters aa, cc, and kk.

This looks like an exponential decay curve that has 'gone to zero...' many half-lives have passed. So, let's estimate cc as the least y-value in the data set. Then, the first y-value in the data set, at x=0x=0, will have the value c+A0e0c+A0e0, so estimate A0=y(0)cA0=y(0)c.

: Note that curve_fit sets all initial parameter values to 1 in the absence of specification to the contrary.

On estimating kk:

No doubt you are familiar with the idea 'after 10 half-lives, a radioactive substance has, for all intents and purposes, decayed away.' In analogy with the concept of the half-life is the concept of the lifetime. After one lifetime, , of the process has passed, 1e1e of the original material remains. Therefore, if: y(t)=A0ekty(t)=A0ektthen: y()=A0eky()=A0ekand: y()=A0ey()=A0eSetting these last two equations equal to each other and solving for provides:

A0e=A0ekA0e=A0ek 1e=ek1e=ek Taking logarithms and rearranging provides the very simple relationship: 1=k1=k =1k=1k Therefore, if you can eyeball the number of lifetimes nn described by the extent of decay you see in your data, you can estimate kk as:

k=ntmaxk=ntmax in which tmaxtmax is the largest value of the independent variable in the data. To get a feel for how much decay goes with an arbitrary number of lifetimes, here are decays for one, three, five, and seven lifetimes.
